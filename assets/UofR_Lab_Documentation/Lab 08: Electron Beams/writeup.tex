\begin{labsection}

\begin{objective}
    To explore the effects of magnetic fields on charged particle beams and to measure $e/m_e$, the charge-to-mass ratio of the electron.
\end{objective}

\begin{theory} 

    A particle with charge $q$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ through a region with an electric and magnetic fields $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ will experience a force
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq: lorentz force}
        \mathbf{F} = q(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}).
    \end{equation}
    %
    The force in eq.~\ref{eq: lorentz force}, known as the \textbf{Lorentz force}, is parallel to $\mathbf{E}$ and perpendicular to both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$.

    Suppose that we have a beam of electrons, so that $q=e\approx\qty{1.602e-19}{\coulomb}${\color{red} (should be negative. So figure 1 has wrong direction. I suggest not showing figure 1 because it's exactly the prelab question 2)}, and we inject the beam into a region with no electric field and a uniform magnetic field oriented perpendicular to the beam. In this case, eq.~\ref{eq: lorentz force} will reduce to
    %
    \begin{align}\label{eq: evB force}
        \mathbf{F} &= e\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}, {\color{red} (minus\,sign)}
        &
        |\mathbf{F}| = F = evB.
    \end{align}
    %
    Due to the vector product, the force will always be perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}$, causing the beam to bend into a circular path of radius $r$. This makes $F$ a centripetal force:
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq: electron centripetal force}
        m_e\frac{v^2}{r} = evB.
    \end{equation}

    \begin{figure}[ht]
        \centering
        \input{Lab 08: Electron Beams/electron-beam-deflection}
        \caption{An electron is accelerated from rest by a constant electric field $\mathbf{E}$ and passes into a region with a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$, which produces a centripetal force given by eq.~\ref{eq: electron centripetal force}.}
        \label{fig: e beam deflection}
    \end{figure}

    To precisely control $v$, imagine two conducting plates with a potential difference (or voltage) $V$ between them. An electron starting at rest at one plate is accelerated to energy $eV$ by the uniform $\mathbf{E}$ field between the plates. Thus, the electron's kinetic energy when it leaves the region of the $\mathbf{E}$ field is
    %
    \begin{equation}
        \frac{1}{2}m_ev^2 = eV,
    \end{equation}
    %
    and therefore its velocity once it leaves the region between the plates is
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq: electron velocity}
        v = \sqrt{\frac{2eV}{m_e}}.
    \end{equation}
    %
    Combining eqs.~\ref{eq: electron centripetal force} and \ref{eq: electron velocity} gives the charge-to-mass ratio
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq: em ratio}
        \frac{e}{m_e} = \frac{2V}{(rB)^2}.
    \end{equation}
    %
    In summary, we can control the velocity of the electron by setting the voltage $V$. If we set up an external uniform field $B$ and measure the radius of curvature of the electron as it enters the region with the $B$ field, we will observe the electron beam curving due to the force in eq.~\ref{eq: electron centripetal force}. By measuring the radius of curvature of the path, we can estimate $e/m_e$, the ratio of two fundamental properties of the electron: its charge $e$ and its mass $m_e$.
    
\end{theory}

\begin{experiment}

    In this experiment, we will use the apparatus shown in Figure~\ref{fig: helmholtz coil and CRT} to carry out the measurement sketched out in Figure~\ref{fig: e beam deflection}.

    \begin{figure}[ht]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Lab 08: Electron Beams/ElectronApparatus.png}
        \caption{The vacuum tube and Helmholtz coil used to measure $e/m_e$ in this experiment.}
        \label{fig: helmholtz coil and CRT}
    \end{figure}

    The apparatus consists of two major components. First, a \textbf{vacuum tube} containing a heated filament at one end provides a source of electrons. The electrons are accelerated by an applied voltage $V$ which sets up an $\mathbf{E}$ field, and the resulting electron beam is collected on an electrode at the other end side of the vacuum tube.

    Second, outside the vacuum tube is a \textbf{Helmholtz coil}, which establishes a uniform $\mathbf{B}$ field in the tube. The Helmholtz coil is made of two coaxial circular electromagnets of radius $R$ separated by a length $\ell=R$. If each electromagnet has $N$ windings of magnet wire carrying current $I$, then the magnitude of the field inside the coil is given by
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq: field magnitude}
        B = \left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^{3/2} \frac{\mu_0 NI}{R}.
    \end{equation}
    %
    Given $N=130$ windings per coil, the permeability of free space $\mu_0=4\pi\times10^{-7}~\text{H/m} \approx \qty{1.26e-6}{\henry/\meter}$, and assuming $R=\qty{15}{\centi\meter}=\qty{0.15}{\meter}$, the field magnitude can be expressed in terms of coil current $I$ as
    %
    \begin{equation}\label{eq: magnetic field}
        B = 0.77\cdot I~\qty{}{\milli\tesla}
          = (7.7\times10^{-4})\cdot I~\qty{}{\tesla}
    \end{equation}
    
    % current $I$ in the coil, we can compute the strength of the magnetic field at the center using the constants
    % %
    % \begin{align*}
    %     \mu_0 &= 4\pi\times10^{-7}~\text{H/m} \approx \qty{1.26e-6}{\henry/\meter}, & &\text{permeability of free space},
    %     \\
    %     N &= 130 & &\text{windings per coil},
    %     \\
    %     R &= \qty{0.15}{\meter} & &\text{coil radius}.
    % \end{align*}

    The apparatus allows you to adjust the applied voltage $V$ and the current $I$, from which you may compute $B$ using eq.~\ref{eq: magnetic field}.{\color{red} (Add reminder: you will measure the $R$ and recalibrate it later)} Referring to eq.~\ref{eq: em ratio}, the final measurement needed to estimate $e/m_e$ is the electron beam's radius of curvature $r$. The tube is filled with a trace amount of He gas, and when the electron beam moves through it, the gas fluoresces with blue light {\color{red} (green light)}, allowing you to see its circular path. A scale inside the tube lets you read off the diameter of the circular path. Dividing by 2 gives the radius of curvature.

    \subsection*{Data Analysis and Estimation of $e/m_e$}

    To estimate $e/m_e$, you will make nine measurements of the radius of curvature $r$ given a fixed accelerating voltage $V$ and a varying Helmholtz coil current $I$. From these, you will estimate the product $rB$ that appears in eq.~\ref{eq: em ratio}. You will need to calculate the mean, the standard deviation, and the uncertainty on the mean value of $rB$. These three quantities are defined below:
    %
    \begin{align}
        \text{Mean:}& & \overline{rB} &= \frac{1}{9}\sum_{i=1}^9 (rB)_i, \label{eq: rB mean}
        \\
        \text{Standard deviation:}& & \sigma_{rB}&= \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^9\left((rB)_i - \overline{rB}\right)^2}{9-1}}, \label{eq: rB std dev}
        \\
        \text{Uncertainty on the mean:}& & \sigma_{\overline{rB}} &= \frac{\sigma_{rB}}{\sqrt{9}}.
    \end{align}

    \subsubsection*{Propagation of Uncertainties}

    When you have completed the statistical analysis of the three data sets, you will calculate $e/m_e$ from eq.~\ref{eq: em ratio} for each of the three accelerating voltages. You will calculate an uncertainty on $e/m_e$ by propagating the uncertainty on the mean $\sigma_{\overline{rB}}$:
    %
    \begin{align}
        \text{Uncertainty on $e/m_e$:}& & \Delta(e/m_e) &= 2\frac{\sigma_{\overline{rB}}}{\overline{rB}}{\color{red}\frac{e}{m_e}}.
    \end{align}

    \subsubsection*{Reporting the Charge to Mass Ratio}

    At the end of the data analysis, you will have found three different values for $e/m_e$ and its uncertainty for each of the three accelerating voltages. You will want to quote results to \textbf{three significant figures}. Below are two proper ways to quote your results:
    %
    \begin{align*}
        \frac{e}{m} &= (1.91\pm0.02)\times10^{11}~\qty{}{\coulomb/\kilo\gram}, & & \text{\cmark}
        \\
        \frac{e}{m} &= \qty{1.91e11}{\nothing} \pm \qty{0.02e11}{\coulomb/\kilo\gram}. & & \text{\cmark}
    \end{align*}
    %
    The following are examples of \textsl{incorrect} ways to report your results. Look carefully and make sure you understand why each is incorrect. If you are unsure, ask your TA or TI.
    %
    \begin{align*}
        \frac{e}{m} &= \qty{1.91e11}{\nothing} \pm \qty{0.0205e11}{\coulomb/\kilo\gram}, & &\text{\xmark}
        \\
        \frac{e}{m} &= \qty{1.9e11}{\nothing} \pm \qty{0.0005e11}{\coulomb/\kilo\gram}, & &\text{\xmark}
        \\
        \frac{e}{m} &= \qty{1.9e11}{\nothing} \pm \qty{2.05e9}{\coulomb/\kilo\gram}. & &\text{\xmark}
        \\
    \end{align*}
